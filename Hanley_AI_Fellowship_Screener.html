Now we're faced with the question of how we want to think about recent trends in major popularity. One way to quantify these trends is to look at major category popularity as a function of the proportion of total graduates who enrolled in that category. If a higher proportion enroll in a given category the following year compared to the previous year, then we could say that the major category has increased in popularity.

We have information about all gradautes and about recent graduates. After checking the data documentation, I started out by making the assumption that in order to access information about nonrecent graduates, we could need to subtract values related to recent graduates from the overall data. That would theoretically prevent us from measuring the recent graduates twice.

However, if you look closely you can see that this might have been an erroneous assumption. In some cases, there are more recent graduates per major than total graduates from the all-ages data. That shouldn't happen if the total recent graduates is a subcategory of all graduates. Therefore, we probably shouldn't proceed with the assumption that removing total recent grads from the all-ages data will provide us with clear information about nonrecent grads.

What we can do instead is simply compare recent graduates to the all-ages data directly. The method won't be as clean as the one outlined above, but it will give us enough information to infer some insights about trends in major choice.

Taken together, these analyses suggest that there is room for improvement in connecting women with higher-paid jobs. Although over half of graduates from all majors are women, majors that have high proportions of women tend to be associated with lower incomes. For example STEM majors have a median income of \$44350, which is over \$8000 above the median income for all majors. However, the proportion of women drops from over half in all majors to about 43% in STEM majors. Furthermore, not all STEM majors lead to high incomes, and we tend to see that women are more prevalent among the lower-paid STEM majors.
